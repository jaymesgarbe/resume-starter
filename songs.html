Album Review

SOS - SZA

This week I sat terrified at how a new album after a hiatus of 5 years could possibly compete with what I consider the gold-standard of modern R&B records, "Ctrl". SZA answered on Friday with an eclectic diary-like record of ranging emotions over ranging genres with a tracklist nearly doubling her triple platinum sophomore release. SZA diverts the path of expectations in such a grandiose fashion that it is impossible to collate these projects. The constant shifting of moods and genres (and the fact that she does them all so masterfully) keep the hefty 23-song tracklist from becoming monotonous. As expected, her off-the-shoulder songwriting amazes on every track through which she is able to maintain a few common motifs throughout. Her lyrics keep the album from sounding like a collection of randomly produced songs, a fault to which many other experimental albums fall victim. Most have thoughtlessly labeled this album as R&B/Pop due to SZA's past successes in this genre. I now struggle to consider SZA solely an R&B artist and find it completely unjust to slap this common epithet on "SOS". This album displays expansive talent in sonic styles never before heard from SZA and I just hope it can sate me for the time between now and her next project. Thank you.

Celebrity Song of the Week

Dallin Corless: Everything is Embarrassing - Sky Ferreira

Overall Rating: 9.4/10

Listen Here

Overall Review

This song has been a favorite of mine for a while and I would give it a 10/10 honestly, except the visuals and vocals are nothing exceptional (although the video reportedly had no budget). Strictly the song however, it’s perfection. Production-wise, it somehow sounds both slightly industrial and late 2010s-indie/bedroom pop at the same time; it’s almost reminiscent of SZA’s “Hit Different” but with a lesser RnB feel. Lyrically this song achieves what I consider to be a perfect balance, feeling both specific and meaningful to the artist while also being open to individual interpretation. The events Sky Ferreira herself experienced to write this song are clearly being chronicled here, yet the lyrics are relatable enough that there is more meaning to be gleaned from them upon further listens as we project our own experiences onto them. The New York Times described it as “counterintuitive and brilliant,” I couldn’t agree more. It succeeds at everything a song could hope to achieve all at once. The bridge after the second verse in particular is some of the most beautiful lyricism and performance I’ve ever heard. I would say this song is pretty experimental, especially considering its initial release in 2012. That being the case, I encourage giving it a few listens before a final judgment to let its intricacies really shine!
